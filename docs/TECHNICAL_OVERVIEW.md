# Technical Overview

This document explains the final `FutureCity_BufferA.glsl` shader from four angles: procedural city generation, path tracing, global illumination, and performance optimization.

## Procedural City Generation

The city is generated from a deterministic grid of cells. World-space `xz` coordinates are divided by `CITY_CELL_SIZE`, producing an `ivec2` cell coordinate. Every major city decision is derived from hashes of that coordinate, so the city is stable, repeatable, and does not require geometry storage.

### Road And Plaza Structure

`IsMainRoadCell`, `IsSecondaryRoadCell`, and `IsRoadCell` produce a repeated road network. Main roads appear every eight cells and secondary roads every four cells with an offset. `IsPlazaCell` creates central and rare local plazas while avoiding road cells.

This gives the DDA traversal many open corridors, which improves readability and prevents the city from becoming a solid wall of boxes.

### City Composition Masks

Two low-frequency layout masks organize the skyline:

- `CitySpineMask(vec2 p)` creates a strong central vertical street canyon and a weaker cross-axis.
- `CityLandmarkMask(vec2 p)` creates several landmark tower clusters.

These masks bias building height, density, glass use, neon intensity, window density, and slenderness. The result is less uniform than pure random tiling: the camera sees a designed skyline with high-rise clusters and a readable central corridor.

### Building Archetypes

`BuildingArchetype(cell)` maps each non-road cell to one of several abstract building types:

- warm block
- stepped podium
- dark slab
- glass needle
- mixed brick/composite tower

`GetFutureBuildingBounds` uses the archetype, spine mask, landmark mask, and cell hashes to compute podium bounds, tower bounds, height, footprint, slender axis, and material style. The visible geometry is mostly analytic boxes plus occasional roof equipment, spires, and beacons.

### Facade Materials

`SetFutureFacadeMaterial` turns a box hit into detailed procedural material:

- windows are generated by a wall-space grid and a box SDF
- window emission is warm or cool based on hashes
- brick color comes from `BrickColor` and includes mortar, dirt, and per-brick variation
- metal panels are driven by structural ribs and panel gaps
- curtain glass is favored on towers, spine buildings, and landmarks
- wood-like accents appear more on selected warm/stepped facades
- neon signs are generated from simple 2D sign-frame SDFs

The shader keeps only three active material classes: diffuse, GGX, and thin glass. The apparent material richness comes from roughness, metallic, albedo, emissive, and deterministic reflection fields.

## Geometry Traversal

The city is traced with a bounded 2D DDA over grid cells in `TraceProceduralCity`. Each ray starts in the cell under `rayPos.xz`, steps to the next cell boundary in `x` or `z`, and only tests objects in the current cell. The loop is bounded by `CITY_MAX_CELLS` and `CITY_MAX_TRACE_DIST`.

This is the main reason the scene can feel large without an unbounded search. The shader never allocates geometry and never stores a city map.

For sun shadows and GI support, `TraceProceduralCityOcclusion` uses a cheaper traversal:

- `CITY_MAX_SHADOW_CELLS` is lower than the primary traversal count
- `CITY_MAX_SHADOW_TRACE_DIST` is shorter than the primary trace distance
- buildings use `GetFutureBuildingShadowBounds`, a simplified proxy box

The visual city can stay detailed while the shadow and GI tests remain cheaper and more stable.

## Path Tracing

`GetColorForRay` is the core path loop.

For each ray:

1. Trace the nearest procedural city hit.
2. If no hit is found, return procedural sky.
3. If the hit is thin glass, shade it with sky plus deterministic city reflection.
4. If the hit is emissive, add direct or indirect emission and terminate.
5. Add shadowed direct sun lighting for selected bounces.
6. On the primary hit, add deterministic city GI and glossy city reflection.
7. Sample the BSDF and continue if the sample survives.

The BSDF layer supports:

- cosine hemisphere sampling for diffuse
- mixed diffuse/GGX sampling for GGX surfaces
- Schlick Fresnel
- GGX normal distribution and Smith geometry
- thin glass as a specialized primary shading path

The submitted static path depth is intentionally modest. Most pixels use two surface hits while a temporal checkerboard subset uses three. This allows real secondary sun lighting and occasional emissive path hits without paying full multi-bounce cost every sample.

## Global Illumination

The shader has two GI layers: true traced transport and deterministic procedural irradiance.

### Traced GI

The traced layer handles:

- primary visibility
- direct sun and hard sun shadows
- secondary path continuation
- shadowed sun on selected secondary hits
- indirect emissive hits when stochastic paths reach lit windows or neon
- glossy continuation through GGX sampling

This keeps the renderer connected to physically based light transport. However, at `c_spp = 2`, a random path rarely finds small emissive facade details. The deterministic GI field exists to make the large procedural city visibly illuminated at low sample counts.

### Diffuse City Irradiance Field

`EstimateProceduralCityGI` is the main diffuse indirect lighting function. It combines several stable terms:

- `EstimateRoadOpenness`  
  Measures nearby road/plaza openness. Open streets receive more sky and road bounce.

- `EstimateBuildingOcclusion`  
  Estimates canyon occlusion from nearby proxy building heights and distances.

- Sky visibility  
  Uses surface normal, height above ground, nearby occlusion, and road openness. Roofs receive more sky; low vertical walls in narrow canyons receive less.

- Warm ground bounce  
  Estimates sunlight hitting road/plaza surfaces and bouncing warm light onto nearby walls.

- `EstimateNearbyEmissionField`  
  Adds local color bleeding from windows, neon signs, roads, and plazas around the hit cell.

- `EstimateFacadeBounceField`  
  Adds low-frequency facade-to-facade bounce. Nearby building color and emission are estimated from the same procedural rules used by visible materials.

- Cheap second-bounce amplification  
  Boosts the combined facade and emissive bounce in occluded street canyons, where real multi-bounce energy would be more visible.

The result is multiplied by surface albedo and clamped to avoid runaway color.

### Specular And Glass City Field

Metal and glass need something meaningful to reflect. Pure random path tracing at this sample count would often reflect only sky or noise, so the shader uses `EstimateCitySpecularField`.

For a reflection direction, it samples nearby cells and estimates reflected energy from:

- road glow
- plaza glow
- lit window fields
- neon colors
- tower height and density
- far city horizon color

`EstimateGlossyCityReflection` applies this field to GGX surfaces based on Fresnel, roughness, and metallic. `ShadeThinGlass` uses the same field for window glass, mixed with sky reflection and sun glints.

This is not a perfect path-traced reflection. It is a deterministic approximation that makes the procedural city visually participate in glossy materials.

## Path Tracing Optimizations

The shader reduces cost through several practical choices:

- `c_spp = 2` keeps per-frame sample count low.
- Moving camera mode reduces path depth to one surface hit for responsiveness.
- Static camera mode uses temporal accumulation.
- Only a checkerboard subset of static samples traces the deepest three-hit path.
- Regular static samples trace two surface hits.
- Direct sun uses a single deterministic shadow ray instead of sampling a large area light.
- Shadow rays use proxy bounds and shorter DDA limits.
- GI uses deterministic local fields rather than many random diffuse rays.
- Specular GI uses a local city reflection field rather than many glossy bounces.
- Dead material branches and unused area-light code were removed from the final shader.

The important tradeoff is that the shader spends computation where it is most visible: primary geometry, sun shadows, facade detail, stable color bleeding, and reflective city light.

## Why The Hybrid GI Exists

In a small Cornell-box-like scene, a low-bounce path tracer can show indirect lighting because the emitters and colored walls occupy a large part of the hemisphere. In this city scene, emissive windows and neon signs are thin, sparse, and often far away. A low-sample random path usually misses them.

The procedural field uses privileged knowledge of the generated city. Since the shader already knows which nearby cells contain roads, tall buildings, windows, neon, glass, and landmarks, it can estimate their low-frequency lighting contribution directly. That makes the GI stable, controllable, and visible inside ShaderToy constraints.

## Tuning Knobs

For faster rendering:

- reduce `CITY_MAX_CELLS`
- reduce `CITY_MAX_SHADOW_CELLS`
- reduce `CITY_MAX_SHADOW_TRACE_DIST`
- set `c_spp` to `1`
- reduce static `maxBounces` in `mainImage`
- disable the checkerboard deep path sample

For stronger GI:

- increase the weights in `EstimateNearbyEmissionField`
- increase `facadeBounce` or `secondBounce` in `EstimateProceduralCityGI`
- increase `cityReflection` weight in `EstimateGlossyCityReflection`
- increase neon/window density thresholds in `SetFutureFacadeMaterial`

For a calmer, more physically restrained look:

- reduce `secondBounce`
- reduce neon/window contribution in `EstimateCellEmissionColor`
- reduce `FutureAccentColor` intensity in facade signs
- lower `sunRadiance` in `EstimateDirectLighting`

## Known Limitations

- The deterministic GI field is an approximation, not an unbiased estimator.
- Shadow proxies can differ from detailed visible geometry.
- Thin glass is shaded as a stylized reflective surface rather than full refraction.
- The shader is branch-heavy, so first compile time can be noticeably longer than steady-state frame time.
- The city is generated from boxes and simple analytic details, so the silhouette language is intentionally architectural and procedural rather than mesh-like.
